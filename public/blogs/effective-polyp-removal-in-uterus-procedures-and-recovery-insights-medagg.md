# Effective Polyp Removal in Uterus: Procedures and Recovery Insights - medagg

**Polyp Removal in Uterus: The Best Non-Surgical Treatment for Lasting Relief**

Uterine polyps (endometrial polyps) are small, abnormal tissue growths in the lining of the uterus. While usually benign, they can cause **irregular bleeding, heavy periods, pelvic pain, and fertility issues**. Understanding symptoms and the full range of treatment options helps you choose the best path forward.

Traditionally, polyps are removed with **hysteroscopic polypectomy**. An emerging, minimally invasive approach in selected cases is **Uterine Artery Embolization (UAE)**—performed by interventional radiologists—to reduce blood supply and shrink vascular lesions.

## What Are Uterine Polyps?

![](https://images.surferseo.art/e2167582-fb5c-46bd-b439-1d31223b7404.png)

Uterine polyps are soft tissue growths in the **endometrium** (uterine lining). They may be single/multiple and range from millimeters to several centimeters. Most are benign, though a minority can be pre‑cancerous—so evaluation and appropriate treatment are important.

## Symptoms: Abnormal Uterine Bleeding

- Irregular or heavy menstrual bleeding
- Bleeding between periods
- Postmenopausal bleeding
- Pelvic cramps or discomfort
- Infertility/subfertility (implantation interference)

If you have these symptoms, seek medical evaluation.

## Causes and Risk Factors

- Hormonal imbalance (estrogen stimulation)
- Age 40–50 and postmenopausal status
- Obesity (higher estrogen production)
- Hypertension
- Tamoxifen use

Your doctor may perform an **endometrial biopsy** if indicated to assess tissue.

## Diagnosis

- Pelvic exam and history
- Transvaginal ultrasound / saline infusion sonography
- Hysteroscopy (direct visualization of the cavity)

## Treatment Options for Uterine Polyps

### 1. Watchful Waiting
Small, asymptomatic polyps can be monitored. Treat if symptoms emerge.

### 2. Medications
Progestins or GnRH agonists may reduce bleeding temporarily; not definitive and may have side effects (cycle changes, mood, hot flashes).

### 3. Surgical Removal (Hysteroscopic Polypectomy)
![](https://images.surferseo.art/749d05d2-eefe-4036-94c7-10e3a2a4bc14.png)

A thin hysteroscope is passed through the cervix to visualize and excise polyps—effective and uterus‑sparing, but requires anesthesia and has procedural risks.

### 4. Uterine Artery Embolization (UAE) – Minimally Invasive Option

For selected patients seeking a non‑surgical approach, **UAE** reduces blood flow via the uterine arteries, which can shrink vascular lesions and relieve bleeding.

#### How UAE Works
1. Tiny catheter via wrist/groin into uterine arteries
2. Embolic particles block targeted vessels
3. Reduced blood supply → lesion shrinkage and bleeding control

#### Benefits vs Surgery
- Non‑surgical access; no large incisions
- Quick recovery (often 7–10 days)
- Effective bleeding control with uterus preserved
- Lower risk of surgical complications

Discuss with your specialist whether UAE is appropriate for your specific polyp characteristics; hysteroscopic removal remains standard for many cases.

## Who Is a Good Candidate for UAE?

- Symptomatic bleeding where minimally invasive bleeding control is desired
- Patients avoiding general anesthesia or surgery
- Uterus‑preserving preference with rapid recovery

A specialist will assess imaging and goals to advise suitability.

## FAQs

### 1. How long does shrinkage take after UAE?
Weeks to months; many notice bleeding improvement quickly. Follow scheduled reviews.

### 2. Is UAE painful?
Usually mild cramping for a few days—managed with medication. Most resume routine within a week.

### 3. Can polyps recur after UAE?
Treated lesions rarely recur, though new polyps may form. Periodic monitoring is sensible.

### 4. Will UAE affect fertility?
UAE preserves the uterus and many conceive afterward, but outcomes vary—discuss goals with your clinician.
