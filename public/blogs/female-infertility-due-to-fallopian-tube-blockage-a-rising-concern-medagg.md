# Female Infertility due to Fallopian Tube Blockage – A rising concern - medagg

![](https://medagghealthcare.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/1.png)

Infertility is defined as being unable to get pregnant after a year of unprotected sex during reproductive age. Causes span ovulation issues, male factors (sperm count/motility), and tubal pathology—one common cause is blockage of one or both fallopian tubes.

Reports suggest more couples are seeking infertility care and facing family/societal stress while doing so.

## Statistics by Sex

- One‑third of couples with infertility have a male factor
- One‑third have a female factor
- One‑third have combined factors

## Why is infertility on the rise?

- Later age at first pregnancy reduces egg quality and increases miscarriage risk
- Sperm quality declines in men over 40

## Lifestyle factors impacting fertility

- Smoking damages sperm and disrupts reproductive hormones in both sexes
- Alcohol adversely affects reproductive function; avoid during pregnancy
- High stress can disturb menstrual regularity

## How Fallopian Tube Recanalization helps tubal‑block infertility

![](https://medagghealthcare.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/2.png)

Fallopian Tube Recanalization (FTR) is a non‑surgical, image‑guided procedure. Rather than open or laparoscopic surgery, an interventional radiologist passes a thin catheter through the cervix and uterus to open proximal tubal blockages.

### The procedure involves

- Placing a speculum and advancing a small catheter through the cervix into the uterus
- Using fluoroscopy (X‑ray) to examine the uterine cavity and identify the blocked segment
- Crossing and clearing the blockage with a microwire/microcatheter

Over 90% of proximal tubal blockages can be reopened in suitable candidates, which can increase chances of natural conception and sometimes avoid IUI/IVF.

Studies show many women conceive naturally after FTR—roughly 6 in 10 in reported series, depending on age and other factors.

## Conclusion

FTR is an innovative, highly successful, minimally invasive therapy with low systemic impact. It offers a path to natural conception for many with proximal tubal occlusion, often with fewer risks and quicker recovery compared with surgical alternatives.
