# Tennis Elbow Injections: What Works, What Doesn’t, and Other treatment options - medagg

Tennis Elbow Injections: What Works, What Doesn’t, and Other treatment options
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tennis elbow, or lateral epicondylitis, is a painful overuse condition that affects the tendons connecting the forearm muscles to the elbow joint, especially near the lateral epicondyle. It’s common among athletes, especially tennis players, but it also affects people with physically demanding jobs or repetitive strain from daily activities.

Many treatment options exist, but when conservative care fails, tennis elbow injections are often considered. This article walks through popular choices like steroid injections and PRP (platelet-rich plasma), compares outcomes, and introduces interventional radiology as a precise, non-surgical solution that’s transforming how we treat persistent elbow pain.

![Tennis Elbow Pain](https://www.gdosteopathy.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/tennis-elbow-pain.jpg)

## Understanding Tennis Elbow and Why Injections Are Used

Tennis elbow causes tendon degeneration, not just inflammation. That’s why treatment must go beyond just reducing swelling. When physical therapy, rest, and medication aren’t enough, injections offer another layer of targeted relief. They aim to:

- Reduce elbow pain and inflammation
- Improve range of motion
- Stimulate healing process in the damaged tissue
- Delay or avoid surgical treatment

Let’s look at the most common injections.

## Comparing Injection Options for Tennis Elbow

- Injection Type: Steroid Injection
  - What It Is: A corticosteroid injection given around the elbow joint
  - Benefits: Provides quick pain relief, reduces inflammation
  - Limitations: Relief is often short-term, may weaken tendons over time, risk of steroid flare
- Injection Type: Local Corticosteroid Injection
  - What It Is: Targets specific painful area near the lateral epicondyle
  - Benefits: Fast reduction in pain and swelling
  - Limitations: May not help tendon healing, risk of recurrence
- Injection Type: PRP Injection
  - What It Is: Uses patient’s own blood cells to deliver growth factors to the injured tendon
  - Benefits: Supports tissue repair, may offer longer-term benefit
  - Limitations: Slower onset of relief, not instant
- Injection Type: Local Anesthetic
  - What It Is: Temporary numbing around affected tissue
  - Benefits: Immediate relief for diagnosis or short-term use
  - Limitations: No effect on healing

## PRP Injection Tennis Elbow Recovery Time

Patients often ask about PRP injection tennis elbow recovery time. It usually takes:

- 2–6 weeks for mild relief
- 8–12 weeks for significant functional improvement
- Up to 6 months for full benefits depending on severity and lifestyle

Unlike steroid injections, PRP promotes actual repair of damaged tissue and is often more sustainable. However, it requires rest, adherence to physical therapy, and realistic expectations.

## When Injections Aren’t Enough

Injections may not be effective for everyone. Some people continue to experience persistent pain, limited function, or recurring symptoms. Factors that suggest you need to look beyond traditional injections:

- Symptoms lasting more than 6 months
- Failure of multiple tennis elbow injection types
- Declining performance despite physiotherapy or rest
- Risk of tendon ruptures due to repeated corticosteroid injections

This is when interventional radiology can offer targeted, non-surgical healing.

## Why Interventional Radiology Could Be a Better Option

Interventional radiology (IR) uses imaging guidance to treat the root cause of pain with precision. It avoids general anesthesia, large incisions, and lengthy recovery times.

- IR Treatment Option: Ultrasound-Guided Tenotomy
  - Procedure: A needle breaks down damaged tissue using sound waves
  - How It Helps: Removes scar tissue, stimulates tissue healing
- IR Treatment Option: Tenex Procedure
  - Procedure: Ultrasonic energy used via probe to clear painful symptoms
  - How It Helps: Performed under local anesthesia, minimal downtime
- IR Treatment Option: PRP Under Imaging Guidance
  - Procedure: Precise injection of platelet-rich plasma to the problem zone
  - How It Helps: Maximizes accuracy and results

These IR procedures are ideal for patients looking to avoid surgery but need more than traditional tennis elbow injections.

## What About Surgery?

Elbow surgery or surgical treatment is typically a last resort. It may involve tendon repair or open release techniques. While some patients benefit, it comes with:

- Downtime of several months
- Risk of surgical complications
- Permanent scar or stiffness

Most patients can avoid surgery entirely with proper care that includes PRP, IR procedures, and continued physical therapy.

## Choosing the Right Treatment for You

The best approach depends on:

- Severity and duration of tennis elbow pain
- Response to previous treatments
- Activity levels (athletes vs non-athletes)
- Underlying tendon damage (as shown in imaging)

Consult with a sports medicine or interventional radiology specialist to get personalized advice.

## FAQs

### 1. Will a tennis elbow injection immediately relieve my pain?
A tennis elbow injection, especially a corticosteroid injection, can give fast pain relief by reducing inflammation around the lateral epicondyle. Many patients feel better within a few days, but this relief may be short-lived and doesn’t address the damaged tissue itself.

### 2. How do I decide between a steroid injection and PRP injection for tennis elbow?
Steroid injections are helpful for reducing elbow pain quickly, but they don’t promote healing and may weaken tendons over time. PRP injections use your own blood cells to release growth factors into the painful area, supporting actual tissue repair. While PRP has a longer recovery time, it may provide more lasting benefits.

### 3. What is PRP injection tennis elbow recovery time like?
Recovery after a PRP injection typically begins within 2–4 weeks, but full results may take up to 12 weeks or more. Because PRP works on the healing process, it’s slower than a corticosteroid injection but often more effective in treating chronic lateral epicondylitis.

### 4. Are there any risks with steroid injections for tennis elbow?
Steroid injections can offer short-term relief but may carry risks like tendon weakening, steroid flare, or reduced tissue healing if used repeatedly. Patients with persistent pain should avoid frequent corticosteroid use and consider other treatments like PRP or interventional radiology.

### 5. I’ve had elbow pain for over six months. Will an injection still help me?
If your pain is persistent and hasn’t improved with physiotherapy or rest, an injection may still help. A local corticosteroid injection or PRP injection can be considered, but in cases of long-term symptoms, interventional radiology may offer better outcomes by targeting the damaged soft tissue directly.

### 6. I have rheumatoid arthritis. Can I still receive a tennis elbow injection?
Yes, but with caution. Tennis elbow symptoms can sometimes overlap with joint inflammation caused by rheumatoid arthritis. A doctor will assess your condition before recommending a steroid or PRP injection. In some cases, physical therapy or anti-inflammatory medications may be more appropriate.

### 7. When can I go back to playing tennis after an injection?
Most patients need to rest for several days after an injection and gradually return to physical activity over a few weeks. Follow-up physical therapy is important to restore strength in the forearm muscles and prevent re-injury. Playing tennis too soon can undo the healing.

### 8. Do PRP injections hurt more than steroid injections?
PRP injections can cause more soreness during and after the procedure because they’re injected into the damaged part of the tendon. A local anesthetic is usually used to reduce discomfort. The healing response that PRP triggers may also cause temporary swelling before improvement begins.

### 9. If injections don’t work, does that mean I need surgery?
Not always. If you’ve tried steroid or PRP injections without success, interventional radiology offers a non-surgical solution. These treatments use imaging guidance to target and break down damaged tissue without the need for open surgery, reducing downtime and risk.

### 10. How do I know if I even need a tennis elbow injection?
If your elbow pain hasn’t improved after several weeks of conservative treatment like rest, physical therapy, and anti-inflammatory medications, your doctor may recommend a tennis elbow injection. The decision depends on your symptoms, activity level, and how much the pain interferes with daily life.

### 11. What does a tennis elbow injection procedure feel like?
Most tennis elbow injections are done in a clinic and take only a few minutes. The area around the elbow joint is cleaned and numbed with a local anesthetic before the injection. You may feel slight pressure or a pinch during the injection, especially if it’s a PRP or corticosteroid injection directed at the lateral epicondyle or painful area.

### 12. Can injections treat both tennis elbow and medial epicondylitis?
Yes. While tennis elbow affects the outer part of the elbow, medial epicondylitis affects the inner side. Both conditions can be treated with steroid injections or PRP, depending on the severity. However, the injection site and type of tissue involved differ, so a proper diagnosis is key to choosing the right treatment.

### 13. How soon should I follow up after a tennis elbow injection?
Doctors usually recommend a follow-up visit within 1 to 2 weeks after the injection to monitor your response and adjust your treatment plan if needed. During this period, you may be advised to start or resume physical therapy, modify repetitive strain activities, and rest the arm gently to support the healing process.

### 14. Can I drive or use my arm normally after a tennis elbow injection?
You can usually use your arm for light activities after a tennis elbow injection, but it’s best to avoid strenuous tasks or repetitive strain for at least 24 to 48 hours. Some people experience temporary soreness or stiffness around the elbow joint, especially after PRP injections or corticosteroid injections, so it’s a good idea to rest the arm and avoid driving immediately if discomfort is significant.

### 15. Will one injection be enough to treat my tennis elbow completely?
It depends on how severe your symptoms are and how your body responds. Some patients feel significant pain relief after just one tennis elbow injection, especially if it’s a steroid injection. Others, particularly those with chronic tendon damage, may need additional treatments like PRP injections, physical therapy, or interventional radiology to fully treat tennis elbow and support long-term recovery.
