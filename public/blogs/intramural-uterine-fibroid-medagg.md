# Intramural Uterine Fibroid - medagg

Intramural Uterine Fibroids are non‑cancerous growths within the muscular wall of the uterus. Fibroids of the uterus are broadly categorized as Subserosal, Intramural, and Submucosal. Intramural fibroids are the most common type and can contribute to infertility, heavy menstrual bleeding, pelvic pressure, and urinary symptoms.

It’s important to understand causes and symptoms to choose effective treatment options. This article outlines key symptoms/causes and reviews surgical and minimally invasive treatments.

## What are Intramural Uterine Fibroids?

![](https://images.surferseo.art/5a1c6176-ca12-4ae3-beb6-94d7879d6418.jpeg)

Intramural fibroids grow within the uterine muscle layer and are often detected on pelvic exam or imaging. They commonly occur in women in their 30s–40s. While the exact cause is unknown, contributors include genetics, hormonal imbalance, and vitamin D deficiency.

## What Causes Intramural Fibroids?

![](https://images.surferseo.art/161f1b32-f7f6-4492-bf04-c415ad3976ed.jpeg)

Fibroids affect quality of life depending on size and location. Common symptoms include pelvic pain, lower back pain, dyspareunia, pressure on the bladder (frequency/urgency), and heavy periods. Possible drivers include:

### Hormonal Imbalance
Fibroids are hormone‑sensitive (estrogen and progesterone). Higher estrogen promotes growth; inadequate progesterone response can fail to counterbalance growth signals.

### Genetics

![](https://images.surferseo.art/24010c71-a327-4ec2-a4f8-bb475998b12c.jpeg)

Family history increases risk. While not purely inherited, genetic predisposition can contribute; healthy lifestyle choices help mitigate risk.

### Obesity
Excess adiposity can alter hormone metabolism, raise estrogen levels, and promote fibroid growth.

### Vitamin D
Low vitamin D status is associated with higher fibroid risk. Sunlight exposure and foods like egg yolk, salmon, fortified milk/juice can help maintain levels.

### Alcohol
Excess intake may alter hormones, cytokines, and growth factors, potentially increasing risk.

## Types of Uterine Fibroids

- Subserosal: grow on the outer uterine surface; usually benign but can press on nearby organs
- Intramural: within the muscular wall; most common; symptoms vary by size/location
- Submucosal: protrude into the uterine cavity; rare but often cause heavy bleeding and fertility issues

## Symptoms of Intramural Fibroids

![](https://images.surferseo.art/38ed4c87-f0ab-4d4a-8681-7ff346bd75d4.jpeg)

### Heavy Menstrual Bleeding
Prolonged, heavy periods with clots are common and can cause anemia.

### Periods Lasting >1 Week
Extended bleeding increases discomfort and disrupts daily life.

### Pelvic Pressure or Abdominal Pain

![](https://images.surferseo.art/9cc7e55f-46b0-4510-a38a-817310dedd31.jpeg)

A sense of fullness/pressure with activity limitations.

### Frequent Urination

![](https://images.surferseo.art/8036fa4b-b4c4-42d6-83b4-dfee6bf03f28.jpeg)

Compression of the bladder causes urgency/frequency; incomplete emptying raises UTI risk.

### Painful Intercourse
Dyspareunia and lower back pain can occur with certain locations/sizes.

## Treatment Options for Uterine Fibroids

### Hysterectomy
Definitive surgical removal of the uterus; eliminates recurrence but ends fertility and has surgical risks (bleeding, injury, adhesions).

### Uterine Fibroid Embolization (UFE)
A minimally invasive Interventional Radiology technique (also called Uterine Artery Embolization). Through a tiny groin or wrist puncture, a catheter delivers micro‑particles to uterine arteries feeding the fibroids, blocking blood supply so fibroids shrink.

## Advantages of Uterine Fibroid Embolization

### Minimally Invasive
Compared with myomectomy or hysterectomy, UFE uses tiny punctures and typically local anesthesia, lowering complication risks.

### Quick Recovery
Most return to routine activities within days to a couple of weeks.

### Preservation of Uterus
Ideal for women wishing to retain the uterus or avoid psychological impact of hysterectomy.

### Fewer Side Effects
Lower risk of major surgical complications and fewer postoperative adhesions.

## Conclusion

Intramural fibroids are common and often symptomatic. Beyond traditional surgery, **UFE** offers an effective, uterus‑sparing, minimally invasive alternative. If you have symptoms, consult your gynecologist and consider discussing UFE with an interventional radiologist.
