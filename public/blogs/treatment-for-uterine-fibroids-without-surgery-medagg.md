## What are Uterine Fibroids?

Uterine Fibroids (myomas) are growths in or on the uterus. Fibroids are common in women of reproductive age and are mostly non‑cancerous; however, they may contribute to infertility or pregnancy loss. Sizes range from microscopic seedlings to bulky masses that distort and enlarge the uterus.

### Causes of Uterine Fibroids

While the exact cause is unknown, factors that can contribute include:

#### 1. Hormonal Imbalance
Fibroids often grow in the setting of estrogen/progesterone imbalance—both hormones can promote fibroid growth.

#### 2. Genetics
A family history of fibroids increases risk.

## Impact of Uterine Fibroids on Women

#### 1. Physical Discomfort and Pain
Heaviness or discomfort in the lower abdomen/pelvis is common.

#### 2. Emotional Stress and Anxiety
Living with symptoms can provoke stress, anxiety, and panic; body‑image concerns may arise.

#### 3. Impact on Sexual Health
Fibroids may obstruct the cavity/tubes and hinder conception or implantation.

#### 4. Impact on Social Life
Sleep disruption, work productivity, and social confidence can be affected.

## Symptoms of Uterine Fibroids

![](https://images.surferseo.art/7832b968-277d-4227-ad57-7bc4475dc949.jpeg)

### Heavy Menstrual Bleeding
Excessive bleeding and clots, sometimes causing iron‑deficiency anemia.

### Menstrual Periods Lasting More Than a Week
Prolonged cycles increase blood loss and discomfort.

### Pelvic Pressure or Abdominal Pain
A sense of fullness/pressure, limiting daily activities.

### Frequent Urination

![](https://images.surferseo.art/5161b46b-b58b-44dd-a4b5-aeba2c43596e.jpeg)

Bladder pressure leads to frequency/urgency and sleep disruption.

### Difficulty Emptying the Bladder
Incomplete emptying raises UTI risk.

### Constipation
Rectal pressure can cause difficult bowel movements.

### Painful Intercourse
Dyspareunia can affect intimacy and quality of life.

## Traditional Treatment Options

![](https://images.surferseo.art/aab73f52-4592-4358-9108-1c209fc1c356.png)

### Hysterectomy
Removal of the uterus (abdominal or vaginal approach). Appropriate for some, but ends fertility and is major surgery.

#### Risks associated with Hysterectomy
- Injury to surrounding organs (urinary tract, intestines, bladder)
- Hormonal changes (if ovaries removed or function affected)
- Pelvic floor effects (organ prolapse, urinary issues)
- Emotional impacts (body image/sexuality)
- Prolonged recovery

## Any options for fibroid treatment without surgery?

### Non‑Surgical Solution

![](https://images.surferseo.art/c42a9d4b-cc56-41b1-aee1-8b0841b73d6b.jpeg)

Medical/technology advances enable non‑surgical care for many patients.

### Benefits of a Non‑Surgical Approach

#### Minimally Invasive
Tiny punctures, catheter‑based therapy, image guidance; minimal scarring and faster recovery. Typically performed by an interventional radiologist.

#### Day‑Care Procedure
Same‑day discharge in most cases; return to routine in days.

#### Minimal Side Effects
Lower systemic stress vs open surgery.

## What is Uterine Artery Embolization (UAE) for Uterine Fibroids?

**Uterine Fibroid Embolization (UFE/UAE)** is a minimally invasive procedure to shrink fibroids by blocking their blood supply.

### Uterine Fibroid Embolization: How it works
- Small groin (or wrist) puncture
- A catheter is guided to the uterine arteries under imaging
- Embolic particles are injected to block branches feeding fibroids
- Reduced blood flow causes fibroids to shrink; symptoms improve

### The Process

#### Catheter Insertion
A tiny incision; catheter advanced via femoral (or radial) artery to uterine arteries.

#### Embolization
Embolic particles (e.g., PVA or similar) are injected to obstruct the vessels supplying fibroids.

#### Removal of the Catheter
The catheter is removed and a small dressing applied.

## Advantages of UFE Over Traditional Surgery

- Minimally invasive (tiny puncture, less tissue trauma)
- Shorter recovery (days to short weeks)
- Uterus preservation (fertility/psychological benefits)
- Lower risk of bleeding, infection, adjacent organ injury
- Often performed as an outpatient procedure

## Why Uterine Artery Embolization is a Leading Option

UFE combines minimally invasive access with durable symptom relief and uterus preservation—allowing quicker return to life and work with fewer complications than major surgery in appropriate candidates.

## Post‑treatment Care Tips

- Hydration and nutrition: balanced diet with fruits/vegetables aids recovery
- Activity: avoid strenuous activity and intercourse for several days per clinician advice
- Support system: lean on family/friends; most pain is moderate and short‑lived vs open surgery

## FAQs about UFE

### 1. How does UFE differ from surgery?
UFE treats fibroids by shutting down their blood supply without removing the uterus. Surgery removes fibroids (myomectomy) or the uterus (hysterectomy).

### 2. Who is a good candidate?
Women with symptomatic fibroids seeking uterus‑sparing, non‑surgical therapy or a faster recovery.

### 3. Is UFE painful?
Cramping/discomfort can occur but is usually well controlled with medications; typically less than open surgery.

### 4. When can I resume normal activities?
Many return to routine in about a week; avoid heavy exertion briefly as advised.

### 5. Other treatments besides UFE?
Hysterectomy, myomectomy, hormonal therapy—choice depends on goals, anatomy, and severity.

### 6. Will fibroids recur after UFE?
Existing fibroids shrink; new fibroids can form over time. Follow‑up with your clinician is recommended.
