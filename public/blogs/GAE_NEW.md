Chronic knee pain can affect almost every part of daily life. Simple activities like walking, climbing stairs, standing for long periods, or even resting comfortably at night may become difficult. When knee pain becomes severe, many patients are told that surgery such as knee replacement might be the only solution. However, not everyone wants to undergo major surgery, and some people may not be ideal candidates for it due to health conditions, recovery time, or personal preference.

Fortunately, modern medical advancements have introduced minimally invasive treatments that can help relieve knee pain without surgery. One such advanced treatment is **Genicular Artery Embolisation (GAE)**.

Genicular Artery Embolisation is a non-surgical procedure designed to reduce inflammation and chronic knee pain caused by osteoarthritis. Instead of replacing the knee joint, the treatment focuses on controlling the abnormal blood flow that contributes to inflammation in the knee.

Because the procedure is minimally invasive and performed through a small catheter, it does not involve open surgery, stitches, or large scars. Many patients can recover faster and return to their daily activities sooner compared to traditional surgical procedures.

## What is Genicular Artery Embolisation (GAE)?

Genicular Artery Embolisation (GAE) is a minimally invasive treatment used to manage chronic knee pain related to osteoarthritis.

In people with knee arthritis, inflammation develops around the knee joint. This inflammation increases blood flow in the small arteries that supply the knee, known as the **genicular arteries**. Over time, this abnormal circulation contributes to swelling, irritation, and persistent pain.

GAE works by reducing the abnormal blood flow in these arteries. During the procedure, a small catheter is inserted into a blood vessel and carefully guided toward the arteries supplying the knee.

Tiny particles are then delivered through the catheter to block the abnormal blood flow that fuels inflammation. By reducing this excess circulation, the treatment helps decrease inflammation, relieve pain, and improve mobility in the knee joint.

Because the procedure is performed through a tiny puncture and does not involve cutting open the knee, it is considered a non-surgical treatment option for chronic knee pain.

## How Genicular Artery Embolisation Works

Understanding how the procedure works can help patients feel more confident about the treatment.

The process begins with an initial medical assessment. Doctors review the patient’s symptoms, medical history, and imaging scans, such as X-rays or MRI scans, to determine whether the patient is suitable for GAE treatment.

Once the patient is confirmed as a suitable candidate, the procedure begins with a tiny access point through a blood vessel. A small catheter is inserted through a blood vessel in the wrist or groin using a pinhole entry. This means there are no surgical cuts or stitches required.

Using advanced imaging guidance, the doctor carefully navigates the catheter toward the genicular arteries around the knee. These imaging tools help identify the specific arteries responsible for inflammation.

After locating the target blood vessels, tiny embolic particles are delivered through the catheter. These particles reduce the abnormal blood flow that contributes to inflammation in the knee joint.

As the inflammation gradually decreases, patients may experience improvement in knee pain and joint function, helping them move more comfortably and return to everyday activities.

## Comparing Treatment Options for Knee Pain

When considering treatment for knee arthritis, it is important to understand how different options compare.

Genicular Artery Embolisation is a minimally invasive, non-surgical treatment performed under local anaesthesia and typically completed as a day-care procedure.

In contrast, **total knee replacement surgery** is a major surgical procedure that replaces the damaged joint with an artificial implant. While it can be effective for severe joint damage, it involves a longer recovery time and higher surgical risks.

Another treatment option is **Platelet Rich Plasma (PRP) therapy**. PRP injections are often used for early-stage osteoarthritis or mild knee pain. However, the results may be temporary, and multiple treatment sessions may be required.

For patients seeking a balance between effectiveness and minimal invasiveness, GAE provides a promising option that focuses on reducing inflammation and improving joint comfort.

## What Happens During the GAE Procedure?

Many patients find it helpful to understand what happens during the procedure before deciding on treatment.

GAE is typically performed in a specialised medical facility by an experienced interventional radiologist. The procedure is done under local anaesthesia to ensure patient comfort.

Through a tiny access point in a blood vessel, the doctor inserts a catheter and uses imaging guidance to reach the arteries around the knee. After identifying the vessels responsible for inflammation, small embolic particles are delivered to reduce the abnormal blood flow.

The procedure usually takes about one to two hours. Since it is minimally invasive, patients can often return home the same day.

Many medical centres also provide educational resources explaining the procedure, including animations showing how embolisation works and guidance from medical experts about what patients can expect on the day of treatment.
