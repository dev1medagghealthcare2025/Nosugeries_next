# Tennis Elbow Pain: What It Is, Where It Hurts, and How to Relieve It Without Surgery - medagg

Tennis Elbow Pain: What It Is, Where It Hurts, and How to Relieve It Without Surgery
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tennis elbow pain, medically known as lateral epicondylitis, is one of the most common causes of chronic elbow pain in adults. Although the name suggests it’s limited to athletes, anyone performing repetitive motion tasks, like typing, lifting, or manual labor, can develop it. Understanding the cause, pain location, and available treatment options is crucial for recovery.

This article explores the nature of tennis elbow pain, how to relieve it at home, and why interventional radiology is emerging as a highly effective nonsurgical treatment for stubborn cases.

![Tennis Elbow](https://www.osmc.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/tennis-elbow.png)

## Where Does Tennis Elbow Pain Occur?

Tennis elbow pain location is typically on the outer part of the elbow, near the lateral epicondyle, a bony bump on the outside of your elbow. Pain may also radiate down the forearm and into the wrist, especially when you attempt to grip objects or lift something with your arm straight.

- Location: Lateral Epicondyle (outer elbow)
  - Common Symptoms: Tenderness, swelling, stiffness
  - Activities That Worsen It: Lifting, gripping, wrist turning
- Location: Forearm muscles
  - Common Symptoms: Weakness, pain on resistance
  - Activities That Worsen It: Using tools, shaking hands, twisting jar lids
- Location: Wrist and upper arm
  - Common Symptoms: Discomfort during movement
  - Activities That Worsen It: Racket sports, typing, repetitive lifting

## What Causes Tennis Elbow Pain?

Tennis elbow occurs when tiny tears develop in the tendons that attach to the elbow joint, usually due to repetitive stress. While racket sports can trigger this, many patients develop tennis elbow through daily arm movements, especially involving the dominant arm.

## Symptoms of Tennis Elbow

The main symptoms of tennis elbow include:

- Pain or burning around the outer elbow
- Weakened grip strength
- Discomfort when using the wrist or lifting items
- Pain during wrist flexion or wrist upward motion

It’s important to diagnose tennis elbow correctly, as similar symptoms can occur with medial epicondylitis (golfer’s elbow) or nerve compression.

## How to Relieve Tennis Elbow Pain at Home

You can often begin recovery with at-home strategies that aim to reduce pain, improve mobility, and restore function.

- Home Remedy: Ice Pack
  - Purpose: Reduce swelling and inflammation
  - Instructions: Apply to the affected area for 15–20 minutes several times a day
- Home Remedy: Stress Ball or Resistance Band
  - Purpose: Strengthen forearm muscles
  - Instructions: Gently squeeze or stretch to improve blood flow and muscle strength
- Home Remedy: Towel Twist
  - Purpose: Stretch and activate muscles
  - Instructions: Hold a rolled up towel, twist in opposite directions to relieve tightness
- Home Remedy: Wrist Turns with Light Weight
  - Purpose: Build muscle endurance
  - Instructions: Use a small light weight, slowly rotate forearm supported on a surface
- Home Remedy: Elbow Stretches
  - Purpose: Improve flexibility
  - Instructions: Keep arm straight, gently pull the wrist downward and upward

## When Home Remedies Are Not Enough

Sometimes, tennis elbow pain becomes chronic, especially if injured tissue has not healed properly. You may need more targeted therapies if you experience:

- No improvement after 6–8 weeks
- Severe pain that limits function
- Difficulty lifting or performing basic arm movements
- Pain during daily tasks despite physical therapy

## Conventional Medical Treatments

If home remedies fail, several other treatments are commonly used:

- Treatment: Physical Therapy
  - Description: Custom exercise program to strengthen and stretch
  - Limitations: Requires consistency, may take weeks to show results
- Treatment: Pain Relievers
  - Description: Over the counter anti-inflammatories or prescription meds
  - Limitations: Temporary symptom control only
- Treatment: Steroid Injections
  - Description: Reduce inflammation in the elbow joint
  - Limitations: May weaken damaged tendon over time
- Treatment: Platelet Rich Plasma (PRP)
  - Description: Uses your blood to promote healing in the injured elbow
  - Limitations: Can take weeks or months to work, not always covered by insurance
- Treatment: Tennis Elbow Surgery
  - Description: Reserved for rare, severe cases
  - Limitations: Longer downtime, risk of complications, permanent scar

## Interventional Radiology: A Breakthrough in Tennis Elbow Pain Relief

For patients who haven’t responded to conservative methods, interventional radiology (IR) provides a non-surgical alternative that’s highly effective and low-risk. Using imaging guidance, IR specialists target and treat the damaged tendon directly.

- IR Procedure: Ultrasound-Guided Tenotomy
  - What It Does: Uses a special needle to break up injured tissue
  - Why It Works: Stimulates healing, removes degeneration
- IR Procedure: Tenex Procedure
  - What It Does: Removes scar tissue using ultrasound
  - Why It Works: Minimally invasive, done under local anesthesia
- IR Procedure: Guided PRP Injection
  - What It Does: Precisely delivers PRP to the affected area
  - Why It Works: Maximizes the healing process

## Why Consider IR Over Tennis Elbow Surgery?

- No general anesthesia required
- Performed as an outpatient procedure
- Quicker recovery and minimal downtime
- Avoids complications like stiffness or scar tissue
- Targets the root cause of tennis elbow pain without removing healthy tissue

## Prevent Tennis Elbow From Coming Back

After treatment, whether conservative or through IR, follow these steps to avoid recurrence:

- Strengthen forearm muscles with controlled exercises for tennis elbow
- Modify repetitive movements at work or in sports
- Use ergonomic tools and equipment
- Stretch daily, especially before playing sports
- Consult a physical therapist for personalized movement plans

## FAQs

### 1. Where exactly does tennis elbow pain hurt?

Tennis elbow pain usually starts at the outer part of the elbow, near the lateral epicondyle, and can radiate down the forearm toward the wrist. Some patients feel discomfort when the arm is straight or during wrist flexion and extension. It may also feel tender during daily arm movements like lifting, gripping, or typing.

### 2. How do I know if this is tennis elbow or something else?

To diagnose tennis elbow, your doctor will assess where the pain occurs, typically around the lateral epicondyle, and may perform simple resistance tests involving the wrist and fingers. If your symptoms align with repetitive strain and pain while lifting or gripping, it’s likely tennis elbow. However, pain on the inner side of the elbow might suggest medial epicondylitis instead.

### 3. Can I treat tennis elbow at home, or do I need to see a doctor?

Mild cases of tennis elbow can often be managed at home using ice packs, rest, and exercises for tennis elbow. Simple tools like a stress ball, resistance band, or towel twist can help strengthen forearm muscles and reduce pain. But if your symptoms last more than a few weeks or worsen, it’s best to consult a physical therapist or doctor.

### 4. What activities make tennis elbow worse?

Repetitive motions like typing, lifting weights, or playing racquet sports can worsen tennis elbow. These actions strain the forearm muscles and can cause tiny tears in the tendons around the elbow joint. Using your dominant arm repeatedly without rest increases the risk. Even holding a phone or opening jars can aggravate the pain.

### 5. How can I relieve tennis elbow pain quickly at home?

To reduce pain, apply an ice pack to the affected area 2–3 times daily, avoid repetitive movements, and gently stretch the wrist with the arm straight. Try a rolled up towel for towel twists or light wrist turns using a small weight. These help increase blood flow and support healing without overstraining the injured elbow.

### 6. What exercises should I avoid if I have tennis elbow?

Avoid exercises that involve heavy gripping, wrist curls with weights, or activities that require repetitive wrist flexion and extension under load. These can irritate the injured tendon and delay the healing process. Instead, stick to light resistance movements with the forearm supported and wrists moving slowly.

### 7. Will physical therapy actually fix tennis elbow, or will I need surgery?

Physical therapy is very effective in helping treat tennis elbow for most people. Through strengthening and stretching of the forearm muscles and elbow joint, it promotes healing and improves grip strength. Surgery is rarely needed and usually reserved for chronic cases with damaged tissue that doesn’t respond to nonsurgical treatment.

### 8. How long does it take for tennis elbow to heal?

With proper rest, home care, and physical therapy, many people start seeing improvement within 4 to 8 weeks. If the injured tissue is severe or healing is delayed, other treatments like platelet rich plasma injections or interventional radiology may be recommended before considering tennis elbow surgery.

### 9. I use my arms a lot at work, how can I prevent tennis elbow from coming back?

To prevent tennis elbow recurrence, regularly stretch the forearm muscles, avoid repetitive gripping without rest, and use ergonomic tools when possible. Strengthening exercises with resistance bands and light weights can help maintain tendon health. Also, vary arm movements and avoid putting constant stress on the same motions.

### 10. Are steroid injections good for tennis elbow pain relief?

Steroid injections can provide quick pain relief by reducing inflammation in the elbow joint, but they don’t promote healing of the damaged tendon. Repeated use may weaken the tissue, so they’re generally used for short-term relief alongside other treatments. Interventional radiology and PRP are longer-lasting options for chronic symptoms.
