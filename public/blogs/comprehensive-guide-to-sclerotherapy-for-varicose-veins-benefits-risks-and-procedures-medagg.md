# Comprehensive Guide to Sclerotherapy for Varicose Veins: Benefits, Risks, and Procedures - medagg

Varicose veins are common and can cause pain, swelling, cosmetic concerns, and complications. Among modern options, **sclerotherapy** is a minimally invasive, office‑based treatment that effectively targets spider veins and many small–medium varicose veins.

![](https://img.freepik.com/premium-photo/knee-joint-ultrasound-doctor-examined-woman-patient_151013-27406.jpg)

## What Is Sclerotherapy?

Sclerotherapy injects a medication (sclerosant) into problem veins, causing them to seal and be reabsorbed over time. It’s also used for some venous/lymphatic malformations, and in selected cases for hemorrhoids or hydroceles. Sessions often take 10–30 minutes; ultrasound guidance helps precisely target deeper veins.

## Indications and Contraindications

![](https://img.freepik.com/premium-photo/depiction-nerve-pain-fatigue-person-with-exhausted-legs-showing-physical-strain-nerve-pain-focusing-discomfort-heaviness-that-comes-with-persistent-aching-lower-body_771426-108811.jpg)

### Indications
- Varicose veins, spider veins, reticular/telangiectatic veins
- Venous malformations, selected lymphatic malformations

### Contraindications
- Known sclerosant allergy
- Active systemic infection
- Personal history of DVT (individualized risk assessment required)
- Severe peripheral arterial disease
- Recent cardiovascular/neurologic events
- Pregnancy
- Immobility, severe allergies, specific coagulopathies

## How the Procedure Works

### Preparation
A specialist reviews history, medicines/supplements, examines veins, may photograph for comparison.

### Steps
1. Positioning (often supine with leg elevation); skin antisepsis
2. Fine‑needle injections of sclerosant into target veins (often from larger → smaller)
3. Immediate compression/massage to empty blood and distribute agent
4. Typically the superficial venous system for the leg is addressed in one plan; multiple sessions may be needed

## Types of Sclerotherapy

- Liquid sclerotherapy: traditional injections for small–medium veins
- Foam sclerotherapy: sclerosant mixed with air/gas, better contact for larger/tortuous veins

## Aftercare and Recovery

- Compression stockings/bandages for 1–3 weeks as advised
- Walk soon after and daily; simple calf exercises
- Avoid strenuous workouts, hot baths/saunas, and direct sun on treated areas for a short period
- Avoid aspirin/ibuprofen for ~48 hours unless advised otherwise

Most resume normal routines immediately; delay high‑impact exercise for a few days.

## Side Effects and Rare Complications

Common, usually temporary: stinging/cramping, bruising, raised red areas, small skin sores, brownish lines/spots, clusters of small red vessels.

Less common/rare: superficial clots, inflammation, air microbubbles (transient visual changes/headache), allergic reactions, skin ulceration. Deep vein thrombosis is rare with proper technique and selection.

## Benefits and Effectiveness

![](https://images.surferseo.art/b003323a-1d58-421c-8990-1c737e67ecf2.jpeg)

- Minimally invasive; office‑based with minimal downtime
- Effective: literature reports ≈50–80% of injected veins close after a single session (multiple sessions may be needed for extensive disease)
- Symptom relief (aching, swelling, cramps) and cosmetic improvement

## Comparison With Other Treatments

- Surgery (stripping/ligation): more invasive; longer recovery; may be preferred for certain very large veins
- Laser therapy: excellent for very small superficial veins; sclerotherapy treats a broader size range and deeper targets
- Radiofrequency ablation (RFA)/Endovenous laser ablation (EVLA): ideal for larger axial veins; foam sclerotherapy can be an alternative, including for tortuous/branching segments

## Ultrasound‑Guided and Foam Sclerotherapy

- Ultrasound guidance improves precision, treats non‑visible/deeper veins, and reduces complication risk
- Foam sclerotherapy displaces blood, increases contact time, treats larger segments effectively, and is easily visualized on ultrasound

## Interventional Radiology: Endovenous Ablation Option

Endovenous ablation (RFA/EVLA) uses heat to close refluxing veins—high success, quick recovery, and durable results for primary axial reflux.

## FAQs

- What are the risks? Mostly minor/temporary; serious events are rare with proper selection and technique.
- How long is a session? About 15–30 minutes.
- Recovery time? Routine activities the same day; avoid heavy workouts briefly and wear compression as advised.
- Is sclerotherapy used for other conditions? Yes—some hemorrhoids, hydroceles, and vascular malformations.
- How effective is it? Many veins close after 1 session; multiple sessions may be planned for optimal results.

## Final Thoughts

Sclerotherapy is a versatile, effective, and minimally invasive solution for spider and small–medium varicose veins, with fast recovery and high satisfaction when performed by skilled clinicians. Discuss your options—including foam and ultrasound‑guided techniques—alongside EVLA/RFA to design the right plan for you.
