# Homeopathy Medicine for Prostate Enlargement - medagg

## What is Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)?

A condition that results in enlarged prostate is referred to as Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH). The prostate contributes to seminal fluid and commonly enlarges with age. An enlarged prostate is not necessarily dangerous, but untreated symptoms can impair quality of life.

## Understanding Enlargement of the Prostate Gland

![](https://images.surferseo.art/9c75911d-ef2f-4cdd-bafb-2cc40a5a8489.jpeg)

Contributors to BPH include:

### Hormonal changes with aging

Falling testosterone with relatively stable or higher estrogen may contribute to gland growth.

### Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)

A testosterone derivative central to prostate growth; accumulation in older men may promote enlargement.

### Lifestyle and health

Obesity and low physical activity correlate with higher BPH risk and symptom burden.

### Diabetes and cardiometabolic health

Diabetes is associated with higher BPH risk; cardiometabolic health and prostate health are linked in observational studies.

## Symptoms of BPH

1. Difficulty starting urination and incomplete emptying (urethral compression by enlarged prostate)
2. Weak urine flow
3. Urgency to urinate (sudden strong urge)
4. Frequent urination, especially nocturia

## Effects of BPH on Daily Life

![](https://images.surferseo.art/ec6a4f99-1b80-47cc-b80c-0d372296dec4.jpeg)

- Sleep disruption from nocturia causing fatigue and reduced productivity
- Reduced physical activity due to restroom proximity concerns
- Stress and anxiety related to urgency/frequency
- Professional challenges from frequent breaks and self‑consciousness

## Introduction to Interventional Radiology (IR)

Interventional Radiology uses imaging guidance to diagnose and treat conditions with minimally invasive techniques—applicable to prostate enlargement among many others.

## Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE): A Modern Non‑Surgical Solution

PAE is a catheter‑based procedure that reduces prostate blood flow, shrinking the gland and relieving obstruction symptoms.

### How PAE Works

- Tiny catheter inserted via wrist/groin artery under local anesthesia
- Contrast dye maps prostatic arteries
- Microscopic embolic particles are delivered to reduce prostatic blood flow
- Over time the prostate shrinks and urinary flow improves; most patients go home the same day

### Procedure Flow

1. Patient preparation (short fast) and local anesthesia
2. Catheter placement and artery mapping
3. Targeted embolization of prostatic arteries
4. Monitoring and same‑day/overnight observation
5. Progressive symptom relief over weeks as swelling decreases

### Advantages of PAE

- Minimally invasive; no large incisions
- Faster recovery versus traditional surgery
- Lower bleeding risk; minimal scarring
- Preservation of sexual function and urinary control in many patients
- Uterus‑preserving equivalent in men: prostate tissue is preserved vs resected

### Who is a Candidate?

- Men with symptomatic BPH not responding to medications or preferring to avoid invasive surgery
- Requires imaging and clinical evaluation by a qualified team
