# Prostate Cysts: Symptoms and Latest Treatment Options - medagg

Fluid‑filled sacs that can develop within the prostate gland are known as prostate cysts. Prostatic cysts are relatively common and become more prevalent with age. While most are harmless and asymptomatic, larger cysts can cause discomfort and complications if untreated. This article explains prostate cyst types, symptoms, causes, and treatment options—including the novel interventional radiology (IR) technique of embolization.

![](https://images.surferseo.art/18653091-1d69-4586-ad03-d7c76e0d47c0.jpeg)

## What is a Prostate Cyst?

The prostate gland produces seminal fluid. A prostate cyst is a fluid‑filled sac within or adjacent to the gland. Sonographic and MRI findings help detect and characterize cysts, especially when evaluating concurrent conditions like prostate enlargement.

Common categories include:

### Midline prostatic cysts (including utricle cysts)

Often incidental and asymptomatic, midline cysts may still compress adjacent structures (ejaculatory ducts, bladder neck, urethra) when large, leading to symptoms. Careful imaging helps determine clinical relevance.

### Ejaculatory duct cysts

Rare cysts arising from or obstructing the ejaculatory ducts; may present with painful ejaculation, hematospermia, or infertility.

### Prostatic utricle remnant

Müllerian duct remnant that can appear as a small midline indentation/cyst; usually benign but occasionally symptomatic.

Studies suggest 5–8% of men may develop small prostate cysts, with prevalence increasing in older age groups.

## Symptoms

While many cysts are silent, larger or complicated cysts can cause:

### Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS)

- Incomplete emptying
- Frequency/urgency and nocturia
- Stress incontinence (leakage with cough/sneeze)
- Increased urine volume, weak stream

### Urinary retention

Outflow obstruction by a large cyst can cause difficulty initiating/maintaining stream, painful retention, and recurrent infections.

### Hydronephrosis

Severe outlet obstruction may back up to the kidneys, causing swelling and potential renal compromise.

### Urinary tract infections

Retention and stasis predispose to UTIs.

### Hematospermia

Blood in semen can occur with cysts involving the ejaculatory ducts.

## Causes

### Congenital factors

Developmental anomalies of prostatic/ejaculatory ducts can lead to fluid accumulation and cyst formation.

### Inflammation

Chronic prostatitis may cause scarring/ductal blockage with retention of prostatic fluid and secondary cysts.

### Infections

Infectious inflammation can obstruct ducts and promote cyst formation.

### Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)

Gland enlargement can distort or compress ducts, contributing to cyst development.

## Treatment Options: Traditional vs Embolization (IR)

While many cysts need only observation, symptomatic or enlarging cysts warrant treatment. Traditional options include:

### Transrectal aspiration

Ultrasound‑guided aspiration of cyst fluid provides immediate relief but may recur; sometimes combined with sclerotherapy.

### Transurethral resection/marsupialization (TUR/TURP)

Endoscopic creation of a drainage opening or resection via the urethra. Effective but carries risks (bleeding, infection, stricture).

### Marsupialization (endoscopic/open)

A permanent opening is fashioned to permit continuous drainage in selected large cysts.

### Open surgery

Reserved for very large or complex cysts; more invasive with longer recovery.

## Interventional Radiology: Embolization

### What is embolization?

An interventional radiologist introduces a microcatheter via a small skin puncture (wrist/groin) and releases tiny particles to occlude arterial supply to the cyst‑bearing tissue. By reducing blood flow, cyst size and associated symptoms can diminish over time.

### Why choose embolization?

- Minimally invasive (tiny puncture; no large incisions)
- Short recovery (often resume routine in days)
- Lower risk of bleeding/infection vs open surgery
- Symptom relief in appropriately selected patients
- May better preserve sexual function than some surgical approaches

Embolization is not suitable for every cyst subtype; candidacy depends on imaging, anatomy, and symptoms. Discuss options with a urologist and an interventional radiologist.

## Conclusion

Most prostate cysts are benign. When symptomatic, several effective treatments exist—from aspiration and endoscopic marsupialization to minimally invasive embolization. If you have urinary symptoms or persistent discomfort, consult your clinician; imaging‑guided diagnosis and tailored therapy can relieve symptoms and protect urinary/renal health.

## FAQs

### 1. How serious are prostate cysts?
Often benign and small; larger cysts can cause LUTS, retention, hydronephrosis, infections, and hematospermia—warranting treatment.

### 2. What is a normal size?
Incidental cysts are commonly <2.5 cm and asymptomatic; larger cysts are more likely to cause symptoms and require intervention.

### 3. What is a prostate cyst biopsy?
Needle biopsy under TRUS/MRI guidance may be used to exclude malignancy in atypical or complex cystic lesions.

### 4. Can prostate cysts be treated?
Yes. Observation for small asymptomatic cysts; aspiration, endoscopic marsupialization, or surgery for symptomatic ones; selected cases may consider embolization.

### 5. Are prostate cysts cancer?
Generally no. Rare cystic prostate cancers exist; atypical features (irregular wall/solid components), rapid growth, or elevated PSA may prompt further work‑up.
