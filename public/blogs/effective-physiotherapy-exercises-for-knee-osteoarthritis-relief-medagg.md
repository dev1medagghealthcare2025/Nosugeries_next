**Physiotherapy for Knee Osteoarthritis: The Complete Guide to Exercises, Treatments, and Non-Surgical Alternatives**

Knee osteoarthritis (OA) is a leading cause of knee pain and mobility limitation. While physiotherapy exercises are foundational, many patients benefit from a comprehensive plan—sometimes complemented by advanced non‑surgical options like Genicular Artery Embolisation (GAE).

![Knee Osteoarthritis](https://images.surferseo.art/9c50232f-4baf-42d4-8b82-5576d3093a47.png)

## Understanding Knee Osteoarthritis

OA is degeneration of articular cartilage with secondary changes to bone and synovium—causing pain, swelling, stiffness, and reduced function.

### Common Symptoms

- Pain with movement or at rest
- Swelling around the knee
- Morning stiffness or after inactivity
- Reduced range of motion
- Crepitus (grinding)
- Quadriceps/hip abductor weakness

## Role of Physiotherapy in Knee OA

Physiotherapy aims to reduce pain, improve mobility and function, and delay progression through strengthening, mobility work, and education.

![PT Session](https://images.surferseo.art/02bfa54c-6047-4b63-8c47-5264ae6c8f9d.png)

### Goals

- Reduce pain/inflammation
- Increase range of motion
- Strengthen peri‑articular muscles
- Improve gait and balance
- Prevent contractures/deformity

## Effective Exercise Program

Combine strengthening, stretching, aerobic, balance/neuromuscular, and functional training. Tailor volume and intensity to symptoms and goals.

### Recommended Exercise Types

- Strengthening
  - Examples: Straight‑leg raises, mini/wall squats, hamstring curls, bridges, hip abductions
  - Benefits: Stabilizes the joint; reduces load on cartilage
  - Precautions: Avoid deep squats/lunges during flares or with medial OA
- Stretching
  - Examples: Calf, hamstring, quadriceps, hip flexor stretches
  - Benefits: Improves mobility; reduces compensations
  - Precautions: Slow, sustained holds; no ballistic bouncing
- Aerobic (low‑impact)
  - Examples: Walking, cycling, swimming, elliptical, tai chi
  - Benefits: Cardio‑metabolic health; weight management; analgesia
  - Precautions: Favor low‑impact modalities; progress gradually
- Balance/neuromuscular
  - Examples: Single‑leg stance, step‑ups, lateral steps, wobble board (as tolerated)
  - Benefits: Fall risk reduction; better proprioception
  - Precautions: Start with support (chair/wall)
- Functional movements
  - Examples: Sit‑to‑stand, stair training, carrying tasks
  - Benefits: Transfers to daily activities
  - Precautions: Avoid overexertion; respect pain limits

### How Much Exercise?

- Aim ~150 minutes/week of low‑impact aerobic activity plus 2–3 strengthening sessions
- Adjust for flares: reduce intensity/volume if swelling or sharp pain increases

### Manual Therapy and Modalities

- Soft tissue techniques, joint glides, passive ROM as adjuncts
- TENS, heat, or ultrasound for short‑term pain relief and stiffness reduction

## Tailored Physiotherapy Management

Assessment guides programming: pain pattern, weakness, stiffness, prior injury, functional limits, BMI/diabetes, and OA compartment involvement (e.g., medial).

### Benefits of Exercise Therapy

| Outcome | Impact |
|---|---|
| Pain reduction | Less reliance on analgesics |
| Strength | Better joint stability/load distribution |
| Mobility | Maintains ROM; reduces stiffness |
| Function | Improves walking, stairs, ADLs |
| Surgery delay | Can defer joint replacement |
| Mental health | Exercise reduces stress/anxiety |

### Practical Tips

- Start slow; prioritize consistency over intensity
- Warm‑up and cool‑down every session
- Modify movements during flares
- Track symptoms/function to guide progression

## When Physiotherapy Isn’t Enough

Consider escalation when pain persists despite 6–12 weeks of well‑adhered PT, ongoing stiffness limits ADLs, injections fail, or mobility declines.

## Genicular Artery Embolisation (GAE): Modern Non‑Surgical Pain Relief

GAE is a minimally invasive interventional radiology procedure for moderate‑to‑severe OA pain. It targets abnormal synovial neovascularity and inflammation by embolizing pathologic genicular branches.

### How GAE Works

- Tiny catheter via wrist/groin under imaging
- Abnormal genicular vessels identified
- Microparticles reduce inflammatory hyperemia
- Pain relief often within weeks; benefits can sustain for months

### GAE vs Traditional Options

| Traditional | GAE |
|---|---|
| Knee replacement | Non‑surgical; no general anesthesia |
| Steroid injections | Longer‑term effect; avoids repeat dosing |
| NSAIDs/opioids | Reduces need for chronic meds |
| PT alone | Complements PT when pain blocks progress |

Best suited for patients unfit for surgery, elderly, or those with comorbidities—or anyone seeking to delay arthroplasty after conservative care fails.

## FAQs

1. How can I tell OA from other knee issues?
- Gradual pain/stiffness with activity‑related worsening; confirm with clinical evaluation and imaging as needed.

2. No improvement after a few weeks—is that normal?
- Gains can take 6–8 weeks; ensure correct form/consistency; reassess programming if stalled.

3. Exercises to avoid?
- High‑impact running/jumping, deep squats during flares; favor low‑impact work.

4. Can PT help with hip + knee OA together?
- Yes—programs can target both joints and gait mechanics.

5. How often should I exercise?
- Typically 3–5 days/week aerobic; strength 2–3 days/week; personalize to symptoms.

6. Is some pain during exercise OK?
- Mild discomfort can be acceptable; stop with sharp or escalating pain and adjust.

7. Can PT delay surgery?
- Often yes—by improving strength/function and lowering pain.

8. Will weight loss help?
- Even modest loss reduces knee load and symptoms.

9. Helpful aids?
- Braces, canes, and shoe inserts can offload and stabilize—ask your clinician.

10. How to know if PT works?
- Track pain scores, gait tolerance, strength, and ADL performance; review regularly with your therapist.
