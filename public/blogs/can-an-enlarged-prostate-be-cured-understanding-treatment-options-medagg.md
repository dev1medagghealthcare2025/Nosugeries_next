BPH is highly manageable. With modern therapies—from lifestyle and medications to minimally invasive **Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE)**—most men can achieve long‑term, comfortable, symptom control.

## What is Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)?

BPH is a non‑cancerous enlargement of the prostate. The gland sits below the bladder, surrounds the urethra, and produces seminal fluid. As it enlarges, it can compress the urethra and disrupt urinary flow.

### Definition and Explanation

In BPH, gland growth narrows the urethra and can obstruct urine flow. The prostate typically grows during puberty, then again from about age 25 onward—a process that can continue through life.

### Why Prostate Size Matters

Growth around the bladder neck/urethra causes lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS).

### Common Symptoms

- Difficulty starting urination
- Weak or interrupted stream
- Frequent urination (especially at night)
- Urgency
- Feeling of incomplete emptying

These arise because the enlarging prostate increases urethral resistance, making the bladder work harder and predisposing to retention.

#### Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms (LUTS)

- Hesitancy; weak stream; intermittency
- Frequency; nocturia
- Urgency; occasional incontinence

## What Causes BPH?

- Aging (risk rises >50)
- Hormonal changes (testosterone/DHT shifts)
- Family history/genetics
- Lifestyle/metabolic factors (weight, diet, activity)
- Comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, cardiovascular disease)

## How is BPH Diagnosed?

1. Medical history and symptom scoring
2. Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)
3. Urinalysis and labs (rule out infection/kidney issues)
4. PSA testing (to screen for cancer risk—overlaps with BPH)
5. Ultrasound and, when needed, urodynamics
6. Biopsy only if cancer is suspected

## Treatment Options for BPH

### 1. Watchful Waiting
For mild symptoms, monitor with periodic reviews.

### 2. Medications
- Alpha‑blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) relax prostate/bladder neck to improve flow
- 5‑alpha‑reductase inhibitors (e.g., finasteride) shrink prostate over months
- Combination therapy for select patients

### 3. Minimally Invasive Procedures

#### Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE)
A non‑surgical, image‑guided IR procedure. Tiny particles are used to reduce arterial supply to hypertrophic prostate tissue, causing it to shrink and relieving symptoms.

- Minimally invasive; no major incisions
- Preserves sexual function (low risk of ED/ejaculatory side‑effects)
- Effective relief often within weeks
- Quick recovery—back to routine in days

### 4. Surgery
For severe obstruction or complications: TURP, laser enucleation, or other procedures. These can be effective but carry higher risks (incontinence, sexual side effects) and longer recovery.

## Lifestyle Changes for Prostate Health

- Stay active (walking, resistance training)
- Diet rich in plants and omega‑3s; limit irritants (alcohol, caffeine, spicy foods)
- Time fluids earlier in the day to reduce nocturia
- Stress management (breathing, meditation, sleep hygiene)

## FAQs

1. Can BPH be completely cured?
- Not always “cured,” but symptoms are usually very well controlled with meds, PAE, or surgery.

2. Is PAE safe?
- Yes—minimally invasive with favorable safety and high success in appropriate candidates.

3. What foods support prostate health?
- Tomatoes, crucifers (broccoli), fish (omega‑3s), nuts; limit alcohol/caffeine/spicy foods if they worsen symptoms.

4. How do I know if I need treatment?
- If symptoms impair sleep/daily life, or complications develop (retention, infections), seek evaluation.
