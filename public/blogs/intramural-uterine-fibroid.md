# Intramural Uterine Fibroid - medagg

Intramural uterine fibroids are non‑cancerous growths within the muscular wall of the uterus. Fibroids are broadly categorized as subserosal, intramural, and submucosal. Intramural fibroids are the most common type and can contribute to heavy bleeding, pelvic pain/pressure, fertility challenges, and urinary symptoms.

This guide outlines causes, key symptoms, and treatment choices—including minimally invasive options.

## What are Intramural Uterine Fibroids?

![](https://images.surferseo.art/5a1c6176-ca12-4ae3-beb6-94d7879d6418.jpeg)

Intramural fibroids grow in the myometrium (uterine muscle). They’re often diagnosed on pelvic exam and imaging and typically occur in women in their 30s–40s. While exact causes are unclear, factors include genetics, hormonal influences, vitamin D deficiency, and metabolic health.

## What Causes Intramural Fibroids?

![](https://images.surferseo.art/161f1b32-f7f6-4492-bf04-c415ad3976ed.jpeg)

- Hormonal imbalance: Estrogen promotes fibroid growth; progesterone balance matters
- Genetics: Family history increases risk
- Obesity: Alters hormone metabolism and increases estrogen levels
- Vitamin D deficiency: Associated with higher fibroid risk
- Alcohol use: Can affect hormones and growth factors

## Types of Uterine Fibroids (Location‑based)

- Subserosal: Grow on the outer uterine surface; usually benign but can press on nearby organs
- Intramural: Grow within the uterine muscle (most common)
- Submucosal: Project into the uterine cavity; can cause heavy bleeding and fertility issues (least common but often most symptomatic)

## Symptoms of Intramural Fibroids

![](https://images.surferseo.art/38ed4c87-f0ab-4d4a-8681-7ff346bd75d4.jpeg)

- Heavy/long menstrual bleeding; passage of clots; anemia risk
- Pelvic pressure or pain; abdominal fullness
- Periods lasting more than a week
- Urinary frequency/urgency; incomplete emptying; UTI risk
- Painful intercourse and/or low back pain

## Treatment Options

Choice depends on symptoms, size/number/location of fibroids, fertility goals, and overall health. Discuss benefits/risks with your clinician.

### Hysterectomy (Definitive surgery)

- Removes the uterus; eliminates recurrence risk but ends fertility
- Major surgery with longer recovery and higher complication risk

### Uterine Fibroid Embolization (UFE/UAE)

A non‑surgical, minimally invasive interventional radiology technique that shrinks fibroids by blocking their blood supply.

- Tiny skin puncture (wrist/groin); microcatheter navigated to uterine arteries
- Embolic particles occlude fibroid‑feeding vessels; fibroids shrink over weeks to months
- Preserves uterus; typically outpatient or overnight stay; quicker recovery
- Lower rate of major complications vs open surgery

## Advantages of UFE/UAE

- Minimally invasive; no large incisions/sutures
- Faster recovery; return to routine activity often in 1–2 weeks
- Uterus‑preserving option for many candidates
- Fewer systemic side effects compared with prolonged medical therapy

## Conclusion

Intramural fibroids are common and can significantly affect quality of life. Alongside traditional surgery, uterus‑preserving, minimally invasive options like UFE/UAE offer effective symptom relief with faster recovery. If you have symptoms suggestive of fibroids, consult your gynecologist or an interventional radiologist to tailor the best treatment plan for your goals.
