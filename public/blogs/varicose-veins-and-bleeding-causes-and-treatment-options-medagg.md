## Understanding Varicose Bleeding and Veins

### What are Varicose Veins?
Varicose veins are enlarged, twisted veins—most common in the legs. Symptoms include visible blue/purple veins, swelling, heaviness, and skin discoloration. While often viewed as cosmetic, untreated disease can cause complications, including bleeding.

Varicose bleeding occurs when a superficial varicose vein ruptures. Because these veins lie close to the skin surface, even minor trauma—or spontaneous rupture—can cause alarming bleeding.

![](https://delawareadvancedveincenter.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/3-1024x1024.png)

## Bleeding Varicose Veins: A Serious Complication

- Fragile, thinned skin over bulging veins makes injury and bleeding more likely.
- Weakened vein walls and high intraluminal pressure mean even a small tear can cause rapid blood loss.
- Venous blood is darker (deoxygenated). Internal bruising can indicate subcutaneous bleeding.
- Immediate pressure is essential; seek medical assessment after hemostasis to prevent recurrence.

![](https://www.schulmanveincenter.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/BLEEDING_LEG_VARICOSE_VEINS-657x372.png)

## What Causes the Bleeding?

Valve failure leads to venous reflux and pressure, stretching and twisting superficial veins. Risk factors include:

- Skin injury over thinly covered veins
- Heat exposure (venodilation) increasing rupture risk
- Prolonged standing or sitting (venous hypertension)
- Age‑related loss of elasticity
- Genetics/family history
- Pregnancy (hormonal + volume changes)
- Obesity (higher venous pressure)

## Symptoms of Varicose Veins Bleeding

- Sudden bleeding at a vein near the skin surface; can be brisk
- Small wound over a visible varicose vein
- Difficulty stopping bleeding due to venous pressure/volume
- Signs of blood loss in severe cases: dizziness, fatigue, or fainting

## Types of Bleeding From Varicose Veins

- External bleeding from varicose, reticular, or even spider veins can be significant

## Why Is Varicose Bleeding Dangerous?

- Significant blood loss if not promptly controlled
- Recurrent bleeding without definitive treatment of refluxing veins
- Infection risk from open wounds
- Chronic venous insufficiency can progress to leg ulcers

## What To Do During an Active Bleed

- Apply firm, direct pressure with a clean cloth/bandage
- Elevate the leg above heart level to reduce venous pressure
- Seek urgent medical care if bleeding is heavy or not controlled after 10–15 minutes
- Avoid tight, constricting clothing over the area

## Treatment Options for Bleeding Varicose Veins

Evaluation by a vein specialist is important to treat the cause and prevent recurrence. Minimally invasive procedures provide durable relief by closing malfunctioning veins.

### Endovenous Ablation (Laser or Radiofrequency)
Heat energy seals refluxing veins under ultrasound guidance—office‑based, local anesthesia, quick recovery.

### Ultrasound‑Guided Foam Sclerotherapy
Foam sclerosant displaces blood and closes target veins, including tortuous segments.

### Embolization (for pelvic or atypical venous sources)
Catheter‑based coils/adhesives/foam occlude abnormal veins and redirect flow to healthy channels.

## Preventing Varicose Vein Bleeding: Conservative Measures

- Maintain a healthy weight
- Exercise regularly (walk, swim, cycle); avoid long static postures
- Take movement breaks from prolonged sitting/standing
- Wear graduated compression stockings
- Control blood pressure and cardiovascular risk factors

## When To Seek Medical Attention

- Self‑care fails to relieve symptoms or appearance concerns
- Any sudden, profuse bleeding episode—seek immediate care
- Signs of a burst vein or suspected infection/ulceration

For persistent symptoms or recurrent bleeds, discuss definitive minimally invasive treatments with a specialist.
