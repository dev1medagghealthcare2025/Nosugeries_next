# Difference Between BPH and Prostate Cancer: Symptoms, Causes, and Advanced Treatment Options - medagg

Millions of men worldwide face **Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)** as they age. BPH and prostate cancer can share overlapping urinary symptoms but are fundamentally different. BPH is a non‑cancerous enlargement of the prostate; prostate cancer involves malignant growth and behaves differently. Correct diagnosis guides the right treatment.

In this guide, we explain BPH symptoms/causes/diagnosis and modern treatments, then compare BPH vs prostate cancer. We also highlight how Medagg Healthcare connects you to top specialists and hospitals.

## What is Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)?

BPH is enlargement of the prostate gland (below the bladder, around the urethra) without cancer. As the gland enlarges, it can compress the urethra and cause urinary symptoms. BPH is not life‑threatening, but untreated cases may lead to urinary retention, infections, or bladder/kidney issues.

### Symptoms of BPH

- Frequent urination (including nocturia)
- Weak or interrupted stream
- Difficulty starting urination
- Sensation of incomplete emptying
- Sudden/urgent need to urinate

Note: Blood in urine/semen, bone pain, or weight loss are not typical of BPH and warrant evaluation for other causes, including cancer.

## What Causes BPH?

- Aging and hormonal shifts (e.g., DHT/testosterone balance)
- Family history and genetics
- Metabolic and lifestyle factors (diet, inactivity)

By contrast, prostate cancer risk is tied to genetic mutations, age, family history, and certain ancestral backgrounds.

## BPH vs. Prostate Cancer (At a Glance)

- Nature
  - BPH: Non‑cancerous prostate enlargement
  - Prostate cancer: Malignant tumor in the prostate
- Cause
  - BPH: Aging/hormonal changes (DHT)
  - Prostate cancer: Genetic mutations; multifactorial
- Symptoms
  - BPH: Frequency, nocturia, weak stream, hesitancy, incomplete emptying
  - Prostate cancer: May share urinary symptoms; can include hematuria/hematospermia; often silent early
- PSA levels
  - BPH: May be elevated but non‑specific
  - Prostate cancer: Often higher and/or rising trend
- Risk factors
  - BPH: Age, family history, hormones
  - Prostate cancer: Age, family history, genetics/ancestry
- Progression
  - BPH: Does not metastasize
  - Prostate cancer: Can spread to nodes/bones/other organs
- Treatment focus
  - BPH: Symptom relief and obstruction reduction
  - Prostate cancer: Cancer control (active surveillance to curative/ systemic therapy)

Both conditions can elevate PSA. Imaging and, when indicated, biopsy help distinguish them.

## How is BPH Diagnosed?

- Symptom assessment and medical history
- Digital Rectal Exam (DRE) to assess size/consistency
- Prostate‑Specific Antigen (PSA) blood test
- Urinary flow metrics and post‑void residual volume
- Ultrasound/MRI as indicated
- Biopsy only if cancer is suspected (imaging/PSA/clinical flags)

## Treatment Options for BPH

- Lifestyle: limit evening fluids; cut caffeine/alcohol; weight and diet optimization
- Medications: alpha‑blockers (relax prostate/bladder neck), 5‑alpha‑reductase inhibitors (shrink prostate)
- Minimally invasive: thermal therapies, implants, and Interventional Radiology options
- Advanced: surgical approaches in severe obstruction

### Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE): A Revolutionary BPH Treatment

PAE is a non‑surgical, image‑guided procedure performed by interventional radiologists. Tiny particles are used to block prostatic arterial branches that feed the enlarged tissue, causing gradual shrinkage and symptom relief.

#### Benefits of PAE

- Minimally invasive (no major incisions; local anesthesia)
- Preserves sexual function (low risk of ED/ejaculatory changes)
- Quick recovery (back to routine in days)
- Effective symptom relief (improvements often within weeks)

PAE is an excellent alternative for men who want to avoid traditional surgery or are poor surgical candidates.

## Why Treat BPH?

Untreated BPH can lead to urinary retention, recurrent UTIs, bladder dysfunction, and upper tract issues. Early, tailored treatment improves quality of life and prevents complications.

## FAQs

1. Can BPH turn into prostate cancer?
- No. They are distinct. However, they can coexist—so proper evaluation is important.

2. How does PAE work?
- By reducing arterial blood flow to hypertrophic prostate tissue, resulting in shrinkage and symptom relief.

3. What lifestyle changes help BPH?
- Regular exercise, weight management, limiting caffeine/alcohol, evening fluid restriction.

4. How do I know if I need treatment?
- If urinary symptoms disrupt sleep/daily life, or if retention/infections occur, discuss options with your clinician.
