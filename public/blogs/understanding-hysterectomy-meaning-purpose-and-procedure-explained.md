## What is the Meaning of Hysterectomy?

Hysterectomy meaning is simple: it is the surgical removal of the uterus. After this procedure, a woman will no longer have menstrual periods or be able to become pregnant.

Depending on your condition, a hysterectomy may involve the removal of:

|Type of Removal     |Structures Removed                                                 |
|--------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Partial hysterectomy|Just the uterus, cervix left intact                                |
|Total hysterectomy  |The entire uterus and cervix                                       |
|Radical hysterectomy|Uterus, cervix, part of upper vagina, surrounding pelvic structures|

Some women may also need ovaries removed along with fallopian tubes to treat or prevent ovarian cancer or reduce future cancer risk.

## Why is Hysterectomy Done?

Common medical reasons for recommending a hysterectomy include:

|Medical Condition   |Reason for Surgery                                          |
|--------------------|------------------------------------------------------------|
|Uterine fibroids    |To stop heavy bleeding or pain                              |
|Uterine cancer      |Remove cancerous tissue                                     |
|Endometrial cancer  |Treat cancer of the uterine lining                          |
|Cervical cancer     |Treat invasive cancer                                       |
|Chronic pelvic pain |Last resort for pelvic pain unresponsive to other treatments|
|Uterine prolapse    |To treat pelvic organ prolapse                              |
|Severe endometriosis|To reduce symptoms when other treatments fail               |
|Abnormal bleeding   |To manage irregular bleeding not controlled by other methods|

However, many of these conditions—especially uterine fibroids, abnormal bleeding, and pelvic pain—can now be treated without surgery using Interventional Radiology techniques like Uterine Artery Embolisation (explained later in this article).

## Types of Hysterectomy Procedures

### 1. Abdominal Hysterectomy

This traditional gynecologic surgery involves abdominal incisions through which the surgeon removes the uterus.

- Often done for large fibroids, uterine cancer, or extensive pelvic structures involvement
- Requires longer recovery and hospital stay
- Higher risk of scar tissue, blood clots, and wound infections

### 2. Vaginal Hysterectomy

In this minimally invasive procedure, the surgeon performs the surgery through the vaginal canal, without external cuts.

Vaginal hysterectomy steps:

|Step                      |Description                   |
|--------------------------|------------------------------|
|Incision inside the vagina|Access to uterus              |
|Detach uterus             |From surrounding pelvic organs|
|Remove uterus             |Through the vagina            |
|Close incision            |Vaginal closure               |

Advantages:

- No external scars
- Shorter hospital stay
- Less pain and quicker return to normal activities

### 3. Laparoscopic Hysterectomy (Lap Hysterectomy)

Laparoscopic surgery uses small abdominal incisions to insert a camera and surgical tools.

- The surgeon inserts instruments and removes the uterus in parts
- Often used for benign indications, endometrial cancer, or pelvic organ prolapse

Types of laparoscopic or robotic hysterectomy:

|Type                                             |Features                                    |
|-------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
|Laparoscopic assisted vaginal hysterectomy (LAVH)|Combines laparoscopy with vaginal removal   |
|Robotic hysterectomy                             |Surgeon uses robotic arms for more precision|
|Total laparoscopic hysterectomy                  |Entire procedure done laparoscopically      |

Minimally invasive hysterectomy options like these offer:

- Reduced blood loss
- Less scar tissue
- Faster recovery
- Fewer possible complications

### 4. Radical Hysterectomy

A radical hysterectomy removes:

- Uterus and cervix
- Part of the upper vagina
- Surrounding pelvic tissues and blood vessels

This is performed to treat cervical cancer and sometimes advanced endometrial or cervical cancer.

## Risks and Possible Complications of Hysterectomy Surgery

As with any major surgery, hysterectomy involves certain risks:

|Complication                   |Description                                  |
|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
|Blood clots                    |In legs or lungs post-surgery                |
|Scar tissue                    |May cause pelvic pain later                  |
|Injury to urinary tract        |Possible during dissection                   |
|Infection                      |Of surgical site or pelvic organs            |
|Vaginal dryness and hot flashes|If ovaries removed, causing early menopause  |
|Abnormal connection (fistula)  |Rare, but serious complication between organs|

Recovery after traditional open surgery (abdominal hysterectomy) can take 6-8 weeks, whereas minimally invasive approaches shorten this to 2-4 weeks.

## Should You Always Opt for Surgery?

Not necessarily.

Many women undergo hysterectomy for benign indications like uterine fibroids, chronic pelvic pain, or abnormal bleeding, when non-surgical options could be explored first.

### Uterine Artery Embolisation (UAE): A Game-Changer

UAE is an advanced minimally invasive procedure performed by Interventional Radiologists to treat uterine fibroids and related heavy bleeding without removing the uterus.

How it works:

- Step: A catheter is inserted through a blood vessel
  - Outcome: No abdominal incisions needed
- Step: Guided to uterine arteries supplying the fibroids
  - Outcome: Targeted treatment
- Step: Tiny particles are injected to block blood flow to fibroids
  - Outcome: Fibroids shrink over time
- Step: Uterus is preserved, no need for hysterectomy surgery
  - Outcome: Fertility may also be preserved in some cases

### Advantages of UAE over Hysterectomy

|Factor          |UAE                   |Hysterectomy                                        |
|----------------|----------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
|Hospital stay   |Often outpatient      |Several days                                        |
|Recovery time   |1–2 weeks             |4–8 weeks                                           |
|Uterus intact   |Yes                   |No                                                  |
|Blood loss      |Minimal               |Higher risk                                         |
|Future pregnancy|Possible in some cases|Not possible                                        |
|Complications   |Very low risk         |Higher risk of pelvic pain, blood clots, scar tissue|

UAE is especially ideal for women who want to avoid the risks of abdominal surgery, preserve their uterus, and recover quickly.

## When to Consider UAE Instead of Hysterectomy

You may benefit from UAE if:

- You have uterine fibroids causing pelvic pain or heavy bleeding
- You want to avoid a major surgery
- You have abnormal bleeding but no cancer
- You have chronic pelvic pain due to fibroids or adenomyosis
- You want to preserve your fertility or uterus

However, UAE is not suitable if you have:

- Uterine cancer, ovarian cancer, or endometrial or cervical cancer—these require surgical treatment
- Pelvic organ prolapse needing surgical correction

## Patient FAQs

### 1. Do I really need to remove my uterus if I have fibroids? Are there alternatives?
Not always. Many women are advised to undergo a hysterectomy for uterine fibroids, but newer minimally invasive options like Uterine Artery Embolisation (UAE) can shrink fibroids and control symptoms without removing the uterus. UAE offers an effective alternative for those who want to avoid surgery and preserve their uterus.

### 2. Will I still have periods after a partial hysterectomy?
If you undergo a partial hysterectomy, where the uterus is removed but the cervix is left intact, your menstrual periods will stop. The uterine lining is what sheds during your cycle — once that is removed, bleeding typically ceases.

### 3. How long will I need to stay in the hospital for a vaginal or laparoscopic hysterectomy?
With vaginal hysterectomy or laparoscopic hysterectomy, most patients stay in the hospital for about 1 to 2 days. Recovery tends to be quicker with these minimally invasive approaches compared to abdominal hysterectomy.

### 4. I have chronic pelvic pain but my tests are normal. Should I get a hysterectomy?
Not necessarily. Chronic pelvic pain has many potential causes besides uterine problems — for example, pelvic floor dysfunction, adhesions, or nerve-related issues. A hysterectomy is usually a last-resort option if all other treatments have failed. In some cases, non-surgical treatments like UAE can help if the pain is linked to fibroids or adenomyosis.

### 5. How soon can I get back to work after a laparoscopic hysterectomy?
Most women are able to return to normal activities and work within about 2 to 4 weeks after a laparoscopic hysterectomy. However, recovery can vary based on the individual, so it’s important to follow your doctor’s guidance.

### 6. I’m worried about early menopause. Will removing the uterus affect my hormones?
It depends. If your ovaries are preserved during the hysterectomy, your hormones will continue to function normally, and you won’t enter early menopause. If your ovaries are removed, you will experience menopause immediately after surgery and may have symptoms like hot flashes and vaginal dryness.

### 7. Can I avoid scars if I choose vaginal hysterectomy?
Yes. A vaginal hysterectomy is performed entirely through the vaginal canal, meaning there are no external abdominal incisions or visible scars. This option is often chosen for suitable cases to ensure a scar-free recovery.

### 8. How does Uterine Artery Embolisation compare to hysterectomy for fibroids?
UAE is a minimally invasive procedure that blocks blood supply to fibroids, causing them to shrink over time. Compared to hysterectomy, UAE avoids major surgery, preserves the uterus, involves a much shorter recovery period, and has a lower risk of complications like blood clots and scar tissue. It is an excellent option for women who want to keep their uterus or avoid surgery.

### 9. What are the risks if I choose hysterectomy surgery?
Hysterectomy surgery, particularly abdominal hysterectomy, carries risks such as blood clots, scar tissue, wound infections, injury to nearby pelvic organs, and longer recovery times. If ovaries are removed, there is also a risk of early menopause and related symptoms. Discussing all risks and benefits with your care team is essential before making a decision.

### 10. Can UAE be used if I have severe endometriosis?
UAE is mainly used for uterine fibroids and abnormal uterine bleeding. For severe endometriosis, treatment depends on the extent of the disease and where the lesions are located. In some cases, UAE may help reduce bleeding and pain if adenomyosis is present, but it is not a standard treatment for deep infiltrating endometriosis.

### 11. What if I want to have children in the future? Is UAE or hysterectomy better?
If future fertility is a goal, a hysterectomy would not be an option, as it permanently removes the uterus. UAE may preserve the uterus, and some women have gone on to conceive after treatment. However, pregnancy after UAE carries certain risks, and it is very important to discuss this with your doctor to understand whether UAE is suitable in your case.

### 12. How will I know if I’m a good candidate for UAE instead of hysterectomy?
An experienced Interventional Radiologist will review your medical history, MRI or ultrasound findings, and symptoms to determine if UAE is safe and effective for you. Many women with uterine fibroids, heavy bleeding, and pelvic pain are excellent candidates for this minimally invasive option, especially when cancer has been ruled out.
