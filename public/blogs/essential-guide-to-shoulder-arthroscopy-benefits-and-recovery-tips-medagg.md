**Shoulder Arthroscopy in India: Cost, Recovery, and an Emerging Non-Surgical Alternative**

Shoulder pain can quickly go from an occasional inconvenience to a daily limitation—affecting your ability to sleep, drive, work, or even lift a grocery bag. One of the most commonly recommended solutions for unresolved shoulder problems is shoulder arthroscopy, a minimally invasive surgical procedure that allows doctors to diagnose and treat various shoulder conditions using small incisions and advanced tools.

But before opting for shoulder arthroscopy surgery, it’s important to understand the procedure, the recovery involved, the shoulder arthroscopy cost in India, and whether you may be eligible for a non-surgical alternative such as PRP therapy.

## What is Shoulder Arthroscopy?

Shoulder arthroscopy is a minimally invasive surgery in which an orthopaedic surgeon inserts a small camera (arthroscope) into the shoulder joint through tiny incisions. This allows them to view, diagnose, and treat shoulder problems without large open incisions. The miniature instruments used in arthroscopy often translate to less joint stiffness, minimal scars, and a faster recovery period.

- Usually performed under general anesthesia (often with a regional nerve block)
- Patient positioning commonly in the beach-chair configuration
- Typically a day‑care (outpatient) procedure

### Common conditions treated

- Rotator cuff tears
- Shoulder impingement syndrome
- Labral tears (e.g., SLAP, Bankart)
- Shoulder instability/recurrent dislocation
- Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) requiring capsular release
- Bone spurs and inflamed synovial tissue

## When is Shoulder Arthroscopy Recommended?

Advised when non‑surgical care (physiotherapy, analgesics/NSAIDs, injections) fails to resolve symptoms or when imaging/clinical findings suggest structural repair.

Indications include:

- Persistent shoulder pain and functional limitation
- Suspected labral/rotator cuff tears
- Instability or frequent dislocation
- Diagnostic uncertainty after conservative care

### Common problems and their arthroscopic solutions

- Rotator cuff tear → Arthroscopic rotator cuff repair
- Impingement → Subacromial decompression; removal of inflamed tissue/bone spurs
- Labral tear → Arthroscopic labral repair; re‑anchoring cartilage rim
- Frozen shoulder → Capsular release to restore motion
- Instability → Capsulolabral plication/ligament tightening

## Recovery Timeline: What to Expect

Recovery depends on the procedure performed. Debridement recovers faster; tendon or labral repairs need longer healing and rehab.

- Week 1: Pain/swelling; sling support; ice/cold therapy; analgesics and nerve block effects wear off in 24–48h
- Weeks 2–6: Begin guided physiotherapy; gradual passive then active‑assisted motion
- Weeks 6–12: Strengthening and progressive functional use
- Months 3–6: Most daily activities resume
- Months 6–12: Full recovery for complex repairs (e.g., large rotator cuff tears)

Pain is expected early but generally less than open surgery. Blocks, medication, and cold therapy improve comfort.

## Pros and Cons of Shoulder Arthroscopy

**Pros**

- Tiny incisions; lower infection risk
- Less pain and stiffness than open surgery
- Shorter hospital stay; faster return to routine
- Diagnosis and treatment in one sitting

**Cons**

- Expense for some patients; costs vary widely
- Requires structured physiotherapy for optimal results
- Surgical risks: infection, stiffness, nerve injury (low incidence)
- May not fully resolve advanced degenerative disease

## PRP for Shoulder Pain: A Non‑Surgical Alternative

Platelet‑Rich Plasma (PRP) therapy is a regenerative, injection‑based option that can reduce inflammation and support tissue healing in selected shoulder conditions (e.g., partial rotator cuff tears, tendinopathy, early arthritis).

### What is PRP?

- Small blood draw → centrifugation concentrates platelets → injection into target tissue
- Platelets release growth factors that modulate inflammation and healing

### How PRP helps

- Reduces inflammation in rotator cuff/tendons and bursae
- Supports healing in partial tears/overuse injuries
- Eases chronic pain from tendinitis/early arthritis
- Improves mobility and function without surgical risks/downtime

### PRP vs Arthroscopy

| Aspect | PRP Therapy | Shoulder Arthroscopy |
|---|---|---|
| Invasiveness | Injection‑based, non‑surgical | Minimally invasive surgery |
| Recovery | Days to a week | Weeks to months |
| Anesthesia | Not required | General/regional block |
| Risk | Very low | Higher (though still low) |
| Best for | Inflammation, partial tears, early OA | Structural repairs, significant instability |

PRP is not suitable for all (e.g., complete tendon ruptures often require surgical repair), but it can defer or avoid surgery for many with chronic shoulder pain.

## FAQs

### 1. Will I move my shoulder immediately after arthroscopy?
Movement is limited initially; sling support is typical for days to weeks. Passive ROM often starts early under supervision; active motion is phased in.

### 2. How painful is recovery?
Expect some pain for several days; blocks, medication, and icing help. Pain is generally less than open surgery.

### 3. What anesthesia is used?
Usually general anesthesia with or without a regional nerve block.

### 4. When can I sleep without a sling?
Often 2–3 weeks post‑op for repairs; your surgeon will advise based on healing.

### 5. When can I shower?
Commonly after 48 hours, keeping wounds protected; avoid soaking until healed (≈2 weeks).

### 6. Do I need physiotherapy?
Yes—critical for restoring motion and strength over weeks to months.

### 7. How long until overhead activity?
3–4 weeks for simple procedures; 8–12+ weeks after repairs.

### 8. Can I drive?
Not until off narcotics and with adequate control—typically 2–4 weeks; confirm with your surgeon.

### 9. Is clicking/popping normal after surgery?
Often transient during healing; report painful/worsening mechanical symptoms.

### 10. What if I skip physiotherapy?
Higher risk of stiffness, weakness, and suboptimal outcomes; rehab is as important as surgery.
