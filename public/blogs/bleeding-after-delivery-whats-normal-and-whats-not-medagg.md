Bleeding after delivery is a natural part of recovery for every new mother. Whether you’ve had a vaginal delivery or a C‑section, your body sheds the remaining blood, tissue, and amniotic fluid through vaginal bleeding, commonly known as lochia.

## What Is Normal Postpartum Bleeding?

After giving birth, your body sheds the uterine lining that supported pregnancy. This bleeding (lochia) typically follows a predictable pattern:

- Lochia rubra
  - Appearance: Bright to dark red; occasional clots
  - Duration: First week after birth
  - Flow: Heavy initially, tapering over days
- Lochia serosa
  - Appearance: Pinkish or brownish
  - Duration: Weeks 1–2
  - Flow: Moderate; occasional spotting
- Lochia alba
  - Appearance: Yellowish‑white discharge
  - Duration: Weeks 3–6
  - Flow: Light flow or spotting

### How Many Days Do You Bleed After Delivery?

- Vaginal delivery: about 4–6 weeks, gradually decreasing
- C‑section: often slightly less bleeding, but similar duration

## When Is Postpartum Bleeding a Concern?

Call your doctor urgently if you notice:

- Very large clots (bigger than a golf ball)
- Soaking a pad in under an hour
- Persistent bright‑red bleeding beyond the first week
- Sudden heavy bleeding after it had slowed
- Clammy skin, dizziness, blurred vision, rapid heartbeat
- A severe drop in blood pressure or signs of shock

These can signal postpartum hemorrhage (PPH).

## Understanding Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

PPH is excessive blood loss after birth. It may occur immediately, within 24 hours, or weeks later (secondary PPH).

### How Much Blood Is Too Much?

- Up to ~500 ml after vaginal delivery can be normal
- More than 500 ml (or >1000 ml after C‑section) is PPH

### When Does PPH Occur?

- Primary PPH: First 24 hours after birth
- Secondary (late) PPH: 24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum

### Common Causes

- Uterine atony (uterus fails to contract)
- Retained placenta (tissue remains attached)
- Uterine rupture (rare; higher risk with prior C‑section)
- Trauma to birth canal vessels
- Bleeding/clotting disorders

Risk is higher with overdistended uterus, large baby, hypertension, or multiple gestation.

## How Long Can Postpartum Bleeding Last?

- Most: 4–6 weeks
- If bleeding persists around 8 weeks (2 months), seek evaluation (retained fragments, atony, infection, or secondary PPH).

## How to Support Healthy Recovery (and Reduce Risk)

- Uterine massage: Encourages contraction; reduces blood loss
- Frequent breastfeeding: Oxytocin supports uterine contraction
- Avoid overexertion: Promotes healing; prevents heavy bleeding
- Hygiene: Lowers infection risk
- Monitor symptoms: Enables early detection of PPH

## How Is PPH Treated?

Initial measures:

- Uterine massage and uterotonics (medications to contract the uterus)
- Manual removal if placenta/tissue remains
- IV fluids and blood transfusion when needed
- Labs to check blood counts and clotting

If bleeding persists, Interventional Radiology provides a high‑effectiveness, uterus‑sparing option.

## Interventional Radiology: Non‑Surgical Treatment for PPH

### What is IR?

Minimally invasive, image‑guided procedures that treat conditions like PPH without open surgery.

### Uterine Artery Embolisation (UAE)

- Small catheter inserted via groin/wrist under imaging
- Guided to uterine arteries supplying bleeding sites
- Tiny particles block flow to the bleeding vessel—stops bleeding while preserving uterine function

Benefits:

- No open surgery; short recovery
- High success in controlling hemorrhage and avoiding hysterectomy
- Fertility often preserved

## Who Should Consider UAE?

- Persistent bleeding despite medications/interventions
- Secondary PPH weeks after delivery
- Placenta accreta spectrum risk or abnormal placental attachment
- When surgery is risky or uterus preservation is desired
